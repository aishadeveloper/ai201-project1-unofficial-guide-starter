KirinoLover (B.S. Finance):
Hey! I'm one of those people that "zipped" through my courses — I just posted my confetti last week, but I did 102 credits in under 5 months.

I didn't have any background in the field I studied, but I did have a lot of corporate experience and general knowledge. I'm in my 30s and I've done a variety of jobs over the years. Because of my background knowledge and experience, classes like HR Management, communications, and all the basic courses were pretty fast passes for me. I also read and retain information pretty well. I had a good foundation to get through some of the courses.

I set myself up for success. Before my term started I did research for each class I had, found Reddit threads with "what to know" and information on the classes itself. As soon as a new course started, I could reference that list and know the best ways to study that course, what was most helpful, etc. It really made me feel more prepared and less intimidated.

In addition, I took studying super, super seriously. I studied in the mornings before work, during my lunch breaks and any down time I had — even if it was only 15 minutes, I was watching a video, reading a book, or taking notes. There were plenty of nights that I studied until 8:30pm, showered, went to bed, and got up at 7:30 to do it all over again. I spent a few hours each Saturday studying, too, and then Sundays I would spend some time prepping for the week ahead. Having meals planned saved me money and time. But pretty much all my free time was spent studying, learning, talking with other students.

The biggest key to my success was figuring out a way to study that worked for me. No videos or music with words playing while I studied, just Lofi playlists on YouTube or brown noise. I use a pomodoro timer on my phone for classes that were reading heavy and it helped a lot with focus. I know myself — if I start scrolling social media or messing around on Reddit I would lose time, so I avoided those as much as possible during times of study. I also never turned on the TV or a streaming service during the day, and I avoided getting "into" a new show even during non-study times because I knew I would want to watch it instead of studying.

I also took physical notes on a notebook with a nice mechanical pencil. It made a huge difference in my retention. I used an entire full-sized 5 subject notebook. I used a couple of different colored highlighters toward the end of my degree for formulas and such. This study method might not work well for you, but it was essential for me.

The thing is, not everyone can dedicate that much time or focus. Not everyone has the time at night, not everyone can focus for long periods, not everyone can type and write as fast as I can.

Don't compare yourself to anyone else. Don't benchmark yourself against my success or anyone else's. What you should be doing is benchmarking yourself against yourself. What can you do today that will help you towards your goal? What can you do today that is better than yesterday? When you stop competing with strangers on the internet and start focusing on besting yourself, you get a lot farther.

My advice for speeding through things: first, familiarize yourself with exam objectives. Then PLAN OUT how you want to complete the course. For me, I'll set my end date and then write down a list of chapters I need to get through for every single study day. That keeps me on track and I hold myself accountable. Also, when I finish a chapter I'll make a Quizlet and review the chapter to commit it to memory. Finally, I give myself 3 days of ONLY practice exams and review before I test. When I say practice exams I mean I'll spend 8 hours on those exams, then use ChatGPT to upload my scores for every individual area with exam objectives and have it generate a report on what I'm struggling with most, and then have it generate flash cards based off the identified weak areas to review.

This is what I've done for the past 3 exams and I've gotten 12 credits in 3 weeks. Everyone is different and goes at their own pace.

eSubstantial-Silver43:
Something that helped me tremendously was looking at all the task requirements first before diving into the course material. That way, I knew exactly what the assignments, papers, or assessments were going to ask of me. Then I tailored my studying to what I actually needed for successful completion of those tasks.

A few things that may help:

1. Start with the end in mind. Before studying anything, open each task and review the rubric. That becomes your roadmap. No surprises = less stress.

2. You don't have to master everything, just what the task requires. This is not cutting corners; it's being strategic. Most assignments reinforce the same core concepts.

3. Break it into small, doable chunks. If you get stuck on small details, give yourself a time limit (e.g., "I'll focus on this for 15 minutes, then move on"). Forward momentum matters more than perfection.

4. Don't underestimate the power of repetition. Quizzes, quick knowledge checks, and summaries help lock things in without hours of extra reading.

You'll get faster the more courses you do, especially once you understand the general structure and expectations.
